Wyndflow

The world's first smart window automation system

IOT

The Wyndflow Economizer uses outside air to help heat and cool your home, reducing AC usage and saving energy. By autonomously opening and closing windows, the economizer provides increased indoor air ventillation to keep your home healthy. It includes a full security system to keep your home safe, and can be easily controlled and monitored from any computer, smartphone, or tablet.

The Economizer was designed to be versatile, compact, and easy to use, while providing reliable home automation to save homeowners money.

Tesla Coil

At 7 feet tall, this tesla coil takes up way too much space in my bedroom. I designed a rotary spark gap to allow for more consistent discharge cycles from the large homemade parallel plate capacitor. The coil is tuned to resonate at around 625kHz, produces 1 million volt electric arcs, and lights up compact flourescent lightbulbs wirelessly from many feet away.

Portable Backpack-Mounted Solar Charger

This backpack-mounted solar panel array was designed for ease of use and portability. After dealing with the inconvenience of a dead smartphone one too many times, I decided to design a solution. Existing products were expensive, and at the time there were no options made to attach to a backpack for charging on the go. Indoor Automated Vertical Garden

My latest project and work in progress. Urban areas are currently very dependent on rural agriculture to meet the needs of their residents. It is well-documented that current agricultural practices are not environmentally sustainable. To combat this issue, I am working to design and build a scalable, indoor, automated vertical garden that would work tirelessly to grow food around the clock without fear of pests or unpredicatable environmental conditions. If this is scaled from indoor garden to indoor farm, urban communities could begin sustainably growing their own produce, people could save money by buying directly from the source, and cities could begin to feed themselves.

Avian Robotics Research - Dr. Elizabeth Smela, UMD (Summer 2015)

I worked as a part of a research team working on the RoboRaven, an autonomous ornithopter (robotic bird). The team was challenged to begin designing a method for achieving autonomous flight based only on input from strain gauge sensors on the wings. By creating custom exfoliated graphite strain gauges and testing the RoboRaven in a wind tunnel, we were able to conduct a frequency analysis of how the wings deform based on flapping speed, wind speed, and wind direction/angle. By identifying unique spikes in the frequency plot, we were able to lay the foundation for how to interpret the results in real-time to adjust for changing wind conditions. I personally wrote all the code for the analysis in Arduino, Processing, and Matlab, and helped teach two high school interns more about engineering design in the process.
